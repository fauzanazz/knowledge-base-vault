# Pricing Models for Software Houses

## 1. Overview

Choosing the right pricing model is one of the highest-leverage decisions a software house makes. The model signals risk allocation, delivery confidence, and relationship intent before a line of code is written. No single model is universally optimal — the best choice depends on project clarity, client type, team capacity, and competitive context.

---

## 2. Core Contract Structures

### 2.1 Fixed-Price (FP)

**How it works:** A single agreed price is set before work begins, based on a detailed scope document (SoW).

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Client has budget certainty | Any scope creep erodes margin |
| Easier to sell to finance/procurement | Requires exhaustive upfront discovery |
| Drives internal efficiency | Change requests inflate admin overhead |
| Clear milestone billing | Misaligned incentives — speed over quality |

**When to use:**
- Well-defined, stable scope (e.g., a migration, integration, or known product feature)
- Shorter engagements (< 3 months) where requirements are unlikely to shift
- First engagement with a new client to reduce perceived risk
- Tender/RFP situations where a number must be submitted

**Risk buffer:** Build a 15–25 % contingency into every fixed-price quote. Never reveal the buffer line-item — fold it into the total. For high-ambiguity scopes, push toward T&M or capped T&M instead.

---

### 2.2 Time & Materials (T&M)

**How it works:** The client pays for actual hours or days worked at agreed rates. Billing is typically weekly or bi-weekly.

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Flexible — scope can evolve freely | Client carries all cost risk |
| Transparent — easy to audit | Harder to budget on client side |
| Ideal for discovery-led projects | Can feel open-ended without controls |
| Protects agency margin | Requires trust and good reporting |

**Rate structures:**
- **Hourly rates** — most granular; common for small or variable workloads
- **Daily rates (day rates)** — preferred in UK/EU markets; reduces timekeeping friction
- **Weekly retainer buckets** — fixed hours/week at a blended rate

**When to use:**
- Agile/iterative product development
- Ongoing platform maintenance and evolution
- Discovery or R&D phases before a fixed-price phase
- Enterprise clients with internal PMs comfortable with backlog management

---

### 2.3 Retainer

**How it works:** The client pays a fixed monthly fee in exchange for a guaranteed allocation of team capacity (hours, sprints, or outcomes).

**Types:**
- **Capacity retainer** — e.g., 80 hrs/month of senior dev + 20 hrs/month of QA
- **Outcome retainer** — e.g., "4 features shipped per quarter" (riskier for the agency)
- **Support retainer** — SLA-backed bug fixes and minor enhancements

**Key design rules:**
- Clearly state what happens to unused hours (roll-over cap, lose-it, or credit)
- Define SLA response times in writing
- Set a minimum term (3–6 months) to cover onboarding cost
- Price at a slight discount (5–10 %) vs. ad-hoc T&M to reward commitment

**When to use:**
- Clients with predictable, ongoing work
- Post-launch product support
- Agencies wanting revenue predictability and lower sales overhead

---

### 2.4 Dedicated Team (Staff Augmentation / Pod Model)

**How it works:** The agency assembles a dedicated cross-functional team (e.g., 2 devs + 1 QA + 0.5 PM) that integrates into the client's processes. Billed monthly at agreed team rates.

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Predictable, high-value revenue stream | Client may try to manage team directly |
| Deep client relationships | Risk of knowledge siloing |
| Attractive to scale-up clients | Harder to staff — team must be reserved |

**Pricing structure:** Sum of individual role day-rates × working days/month, with a management/coordination fee (10–15 %) on top. Offer a team discount of 8–12 % vs. individually contracted roles.

**When to use:**
- Clients who need sustained velocity but can't hire in-house
- Product companies scaling fast
- Clients in multiple time zones who need consistent ownership

---

### 2.5 Capped T&M

**How it works:** A T&M engagement with a hard spend ceiling. The agency bills actuals up to the cap; anything beyond the cap is absorbed by the agency unless a scope-change order is raised.

This is a hybrid that balances client budget certainty with agency flexibility during execution. It is increasingly preferred by mid-market clients.

**Design tips:**
- Set the cap at the 80th-percentile confidence estimate (not the median)
- Include a explicit scope definition and change-order clause
- Track budget burn weekly and surface it on dashboards shared with the client

---

### 2.6 Cost-Plus

**How it works:** The agency invoices verified costs (salaries, tooling, subcontractors) and adds an agreed markup percentage (e.g., cost + 30 %).

**When to use:**
- Government and public-sector contracts that require open-book accounting
- Large enterprise MSAs with procurement audit requirements
- R&D grant-funded projects with compliance constraints

**Pitfall:** Transparent cost exposure can trigger client attempts to reduce markup over time or bring work in-house. Protect with minimum-term clauses.

---

### 2.7 Outcome-Based / Value-Based Pricing

**How it works:** Pricing is tied to business results delivered — revenue uplift, cost savings, conversion rate improvements — rather than effort expended.

**Examples:**
- "We charge £40k + 2 % of incremental revenue generated in the first 12 months."
- "Our fee is £60k; if we hit the agreed KPIs we earn a £20k bonus."

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Aligns agency incentives with client outcomes | Attribution is difficult to agree |
| Can yield significantly higher revenue | Requires robust measurement framework |
| Differentiates from commodity competitors | Risky if external factors affect KPIs |

**When to use:**
- Mature client relationships with established trust
- Projects with clear, measurable ROI (e-commerce, lead-gen, automation)
- Agencies with strong domain expertise and confidence in their impact

---

### 2.8 Revenue Share

**How it works:** The agency takes a reduced (or zero) upfront fee in exchange for an equity stake or a percentage of future revenues from the product built.

**Structure considerations:**
- Define revenue clearly (gross vs. net, recurring vs. one-off)
- Set a cap on total payout (e.g., 3× of notional project value)
- Include sunset clauses (e.g., share terminates after 3 years or on acquisition)
- Require board observer rights or at minimum financial reporting rights

**Risk:** Cash flow exposure can be significant. Limit revenue-share deals to < 10 % of total pipeline unless the agency has a venture arm.

## 3. Rate Card Design

### 3.1 Role-Based Rate Tiers

Define rates by seniority and discipline. Example structure (GBP day rates, UK market, 2026):

| Role | Junior | Mid | Senior | Lead/Principal |
|------|--------|-----|--------|----------------|
| Backend Engineer | £350 | £475 | £625 | £775 |
| Frontend Engineer | £325 | £450 | £600 | £750 |
| Mobile (iOS/Android) | £375 | £500 | £650 | £800 |
| DevOps / Platform | £400 | £525 | £675 | £825 |
| UX/UI Designer | £300 | £425 | £575 | £700 |
| QA Engineer | £275 | £375 | £500 | £625 |
| Product Manager | £350 | £500 | £650 | £800 |
| Solution Architect | — | — | £750 | £950 |

> Adjust for geography: US rates run ~40–60 % higher; Eastern Europe ~40–50 % lower.

### 3.2 Blended Rates

A **blended rate** is a single composite day-rate for a mixed team, simplifying client invoicing.

**Formula:**
```
Blended Rate = Σ(Role Rate × Allocation) / Total Allocation
```

**Example:** 2× Senior Dev (£625) + 1× Mid QA (£375) + 0.5× PM (£500)
```
= (2×625 + 1×375 + 0.5×500) / 3.5
= (1250 + 375 + 250) / 3.5
= £1875 / 3.5 ≈ £536/day blended
```

Present blended rates to clients who find per-role billing complex. Retain the role breakdown internally for margin tracking.

### 3.3 Rate Card Versioning

- Issue a new rate card annually (Q1); notify existing clients 60–90 days in advance
- Grandfather active contracts for the remainder of the current term
- Typical annual rate increase: 4–7 % (track against CPI + tech salary inflation)
- Maintain a "public" rate card and a "negotiated" rate card for key accounts

## 4. Margin Targets

| Revenue Band | Target Gross Margin | Notes |
|-------------|---------------------|-------|
| < £500k ARR | 45–55 % | High owner involvement; lower overhead absorption |
| £500k–£2M ARR | 40–50 % | Sales and PM headcount coming online |
| £2M–£10M ARR | 35–45 % | Infrastructure and management layers thicken |
| > £10M ARR | 30–40 % | Scale efficiency; larger account discounts |

**EBITDA target:** 15–25 % for a well-run software house.

**Cost benchmarks:**
- Direct labour (salaries of billable staff): 45–55 % of revenue
- Sales & marketing: 8–12 % of revenue
- G&A (finance, HR, ops): 6–10 % of revenue
- R&D / IP investment: 3–6 % of revenue

## 5. Pricing Psychology

### 5.1 Anchoring

Present a high-tier option first. The initial number anchors client perception of value before they see lower options. A three-tier proposal (Premium → Standard → Essentials) reliably shifts selections toward the middle tier — which should be your preferred margin option.

### 5.2 Decoy Pricing

Introduce a "decoy" option that is clearly inferior value to the target tier but close in price to the economy tier. This makes the target tier appear more rational by comparison.

**Example:**
- Essentials: £28k (limited scope, no dedicated PM)
- **Standard: £45k** ← target
- Decoy: £40k (same scope as Standard but no post-launch support)
- Premium: £70k (full managed service)

### 5.3 Charm Pricing & Rounding

- Use rounded numbers for B2B/enterprise (£50,000 signals confidence; £49,750 signals penny-pinching)
- Use odd-number pricing for SMB/startup tiers (£4,950/month feels cheaper than £5,000)

### 5.4 Price-to-Value Framing

Always anchor your price to the client's expected business outcome, not your effort:
> "This £80k investment is expected to automate a process currently costing you £240k/year in staff time — a 3× ROI in year one."

## 6. Competitive Analysis & Discounting

### 6.1 Competitive Positioning

| Segment | Typical Day-Rate Range (UK) | Positioning |
|---------|----------------------------|-------------|
| Offshore (India/Eastern EU) | £100–£250 | Cost; may need extra oversight |
| Nearshore (Poland/Romania) | £250–£450 | Cost + quality balance |
| UK boutique agency | £450–£750 | Quality, domain expertise |
| UK tier-1 consultancy | £750–£1,500+ | Brand, compliance, scale |

Position your agency by specialisation, not by rate alone. Competing on price against offshore is a race to the bottom.

### 6.2 Discounting Rules

- **Never discount the rate card without a reason** — "I'll give you 10 % off" devalues your brand
- Acceptable discount triggers: volume commitment, early payment, multi-project deal, reference permission, case study rights
- Maximum discretionary discount: 10–15 % for sales team without director approval
- Offer non-cash concessions first: extended payment terms, additional discovery hours, free training session

### 6.3 Competitive Intelligence

- Benchmark against published rate surveys (e.g., Clutch, Accelerance, Commit)
- Win/loss interview every lost deal — understand whether price was the stated or real reason
- Track competitor job postings to infer their cost base and hiring trajectory

## 7. Market-Segment Pricing

### 7.1 Enterprise

- Longer sales cycle (3–9 months); budget approval multi-layered
- Procurement will demand SoW, MSA, and often cost-plus transparency
- Rate cards must align with vendor classification tiers
- Consider managed-service wrappers to embed support SLAs and increase ACV
- Payment terms: Net 30–60; negotiate milestone billing to protect cash flow

### 7.2 Startups & Scale-ups

- Budget-sensitive but time-sensitive; speed of delivery is key differentiator
- Offer phased scopes (MVP phase + growth phase) with clear off-ramps
- Monthly retainer or sprint-based T&M preferred
- Consider equity kickers on moonshot projects (with appropriate legal protection)
- Payment terms: Net 15 or weekly; require deposits (25–50 % upfront)

### 7.3 Government & Public Sector

- Must comply with procurement frameworks (G-Cloud, DOS in UK; GSA in US)
- Framework rates are often capped — factor this into team allocation decisions
- Cost-plus or FP preferred; outcome-based is rare
- Expect slow payment (Net 30–60+); model cash flow accordingly
- Certifications (ISO 27001, Cyber Essentials, SC clearance) unlock higher-value opportunities

## 8. Proposal Pricing Templates

### 8.1 Fixed-Price Proposal Structure

```
1. Executive Summary (value, not features)
2. Scope of Work
   - Included
   - Explicitly excluded (critical)
3. Delivery Approach & Timeline
4. Team Composition
5. Investment Summary
   ├── Phase 1 Discovery:    £X
   ├── Phase 2 Build:        £X
   ├── Phase 3 Launch:       £X
   └── Total:                £X
6. Payment Schedule (milestone-linked)
7. Assumptions & Dependencies
8. Change Control Process
9. Terms & Conditions reference
```

### 8.2 T&M / Retainer Proposal Structure

```
1. Executive Summary
2. Engagement Model (T&M / retainer)
3. Team & Rate Card
   ├── Role | Seniority | Day Rate | Est. Days/Month
4. Estimated Monthly Investment (low / likely / high range)
5. Sprint / Reporting Cadence
6. Out-of-scope triggers (what generates a change order)
7. Minimum term & notice period
8. Payment Terms
```

### 8.3 Pricing Hygiene Checklist

- [ ] Contingency buffer included (FP) or cap set (capped T&M)
- [ ] Assumptions and exclusions explicitly listed
- [ ] Change-order clause included
- [ ] Payment milestones or schedule defined
- [ ] Escalation clause for multi-year contracts (CPI + X %)
- [ ] IP ownership and licensing terms referenced
- [ ] Deposit / retainer upfront payment required
- [ ] Proposal validity window stated (e.g., valid for 30 days)

## 9. Quick Reference — Model Selection Matrix

| Situation | Recommended Model |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Well-defined, short scope | Fixed-Price |
| Evolving scope, agile delivery | T&M |
| Client needs budget certainty + flexibility | Capped T&M |
| Ongoing product development | Retainer or Dedicated Team |
| Government / open-book required | Cost-Plus |
| High ROI, measurable outcome | Value-Based + bonus |
| Early-stage startup, limited cash | Revenue Share (with caution) |
| Post-launch support & maintenance | Support Retainer |
