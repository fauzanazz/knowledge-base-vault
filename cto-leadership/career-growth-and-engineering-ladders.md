# Career Growth and Engineering Ladders

## Why Career Frameworks Matter

Engineering career frameworks solve three problems simultaneously:
1. **Retention** — Engineers leave when they can't see a path forward
2. **Calibration** — Without shared language, promotions become political
3. **Hiring** — Candidates evaluate your company by how clearly you define growth

A framework is NOT a checklist. It's a **shared vocabulary** for discussing expectations, identifying gaps, and celebrating strengths.

## Anatomy of a Growth Framework

### Core Dimensions

Most mature frameworks evaluate engineers across 4-6 dimensions:

| Dimension | What It Measures | Example Behaviors |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Technology** | Technical depth, architecture decisions | "Designs systems that handle 10x current load" |
| **System** | Operational thinking, reliability | "Reduces on-call incidents by improving observability" |
| **People** | Mentoring, collaboration, influence | "Mentors 2+ junior engineers; runs knowledge-sharing sessions" |
| **Process** | Delivery, planning, methodology | "Breaks epics into well-scoped tickets with clear acceptance criteria" |
| **Influence** | Scope of impact, leadership | "Influences technical direction across multiple teams" |

### Level Progression

Typical IC ladder (varies by company):

```
L1  Junior Engineer     → Learning, executing tasks
L2  Mid-level Engineer  → Independent contributor, owns features
L3  Senior Engineer     → Owns systems, mentors others
L4  Staff Engineer      → Cross-team influence, technical strategy
L5  Principal Engineer  → Org-wide impact, industry recognition
L6  Distinguished       → Company-defining technical vision
```

**Key insight from ProntoPro:** Each level should have a clear "what good looks like" description, not just requirements. Engineers should be able to self-assess where they are without manager interpretation.

### The IC vs Management Fork

The fork typically happens at L3/Senior level:

```
                    ┌─ Staff → Principal → Distinguished (IC Track)
Junior → Mid → Senior ─┤
                    └─ Engineering Manager → Director → VP Eng (Management Track)
```

**Charity Majors' Engineer/Manager Pendulum:** The fork is NOT permanent. The best engineering leaders oscillate between IC and management roles throughout their careers. Each stint enriches the next:
- IC → Management: You understand what engineers actually need
- Management → IC: You understand organizational constraints

**Anti-pattern:** Treating management as a "promotion" from IC. It's a **career change**, not a level-up.

- **System** — System design and ownership
- **People** — Relationship and mentoring
- **Process** — Delivery and methodology

Each axis scored 1-5. Different roles have different expected shapes:
- **Tech Lead:** High Technology + System, moderate People

## The Reverse Interview

### Why It Matters

Career decisions have **asymmetric consequences** — a bad hire costs a company time and money, but a bad career move costs YOU years. The reverse interview puts you in control.

### Framework (Reforge / Elena Verna)

**Before the interview:**
1. Define your **non-negotiables** (remote? equity? specific tech stack?)
2. Research the company's **stage** (pre-PMF, scaling, mature)
3. Identify what YOU need to learn in your next role

**Key questions by category:**

#### Company Health
- "What's your runway? When do you plan to raise next?"
- "What's your current revenue growth rate?"
- "How many customers churned last quarter?"

#### Engineering Culture
- "Walk me through your last production incident — how was it handled?"
- "How do you decide what to build next?"
- "What's your deploy frequency?"
- "Show me a recent PR review — what does your feedback culture look like?"

#### Growth Opportunity
- "What would success look like for this role in 6 months?"
- "Who was the last person promoted from this level? What did they do?"
- "What's the biggest technical challenge you're facing right now?"

#### Red Flags to Watch For
- Vague answers about metrics ("we're growing fast")
- No clear career ladder or growth framework
- "We're like a family" (boundaries issue)
- Can't articulate why the last person left
- Interview process has no technical depth

### The Decision Matrix

Score each opportunity on:
1. **Learning velocity** — How fast will you grow?
2. **Impact potential** — Can you move metrics that matter?
3. **Team quality** — Will you be the smartest or learn from others?
4. **Compensation** — Total comp including equity upside
5. **Mission alignment** — Do you care about what they're building?

Weight by your current career stage:
- **Early career:** Learning velocity > everything
- **Mid career:** Impact potential + Team quality
- **Late career:** Mission alignment + Compensation

## Building Your Framework (For CTOs)

### Step 1: Audit Current State
- Interview engineers: "Do you know what's expected for promotion?"
- Check: Are promotions consistent across teams?
- Identify: Where do engineers get stuck?

### Step 2: Design Dimensions
- Start with 4 dimensions max (Technology, People, Process, Influence)
- Write behavioral anchors, not skill lists
- Include "anti-patterns" at each level

### Step 3: Calibrate
- Run calibration sessions across managers
- Use real engineer examples (anonymized)
- Adjust framework based on disagreements

### Step 4: Roll Out
- Train managers first, then engineers
- Make it a living document (review quarterly)
- Tie to compensation bands (critical for trust)

## Key Takeaways

1. **Career ladders are retention tools**, not bureaucracy — invest in them early
2. **IC and management are parallel tracks**, not a hierarchy
3. **The reverse interview is your most powerful career tool** — use it every time
4. **Behavioral anchors > skill checklists** — show what good looks like
5. **Calibration is the hard part** — the framework is worthless without cross-team alignment
6. **Review quarterly** — stale frameworks breed cynicism
